日子很快过去，医生和他们分别，返回法兰克福。冬天来了，山里的雪很大，Heidi和爷爷搬到了村子里的房子里，白天Heidi上学，傍晚Peter会来找她，等雪停出太阳后，Heidi终于又去见祖母了。


Heidi had approached the bed, asking anxiously( 焦急地 ),

“Are you very sick, grandmother?”

“No, no, child,” the old woman told her,

“but the frost( 霜 ) has gone into my body a little.”

“But, grandmother, your bed is lower at your head’s side, where it ought to be high.

No bed ought to be like that.”

“I know, child, I can feel it well.” So saying,

the old woman tried to change her position on the pillow( 枕头 ) that lay under her like a thin board.

“My pillow never was very thick, and sleeping on it all these years has made it flat.”

“Oh dear, if I had only asked Clara to give me the bed I had in Frankfurt!” Heidi said sadly.

“It had three big pillows on it;

I could hardly sleep because I kept sliding( 滑 ) down from them all the time.

Could you sleep with them, grandmother?”

“Of course, because that would keep me warm.

I could breathe( 呼吸 ) so much easier, too,” said the grandmother, trying to find a higher place to lie on.

“But I must not talk about it anymore, for I have to be thankful for many things.

I get the lovely roll( 面包卷 ) every day and have this beautiful warm scarf.

I also have you, my child!

Heidi, wouldn’t you like to read me something today?”

Heidi immediately fetched( 拿 ) the book and read one song after another.

While she was reading, the grandmother was lying with folded( 交叠的 ) hands.

Her face, which had been so sad a short time ago, was lit up with a happy smile.

When she had finished reading, the child said good night.

Pulling Peter with her, she ran outside.

The bright moon was shining down on the white snow, light as day.

Peter had prepared a sled( 雪橇 ) to take Heidi home.

The two children were already flying down the Alp, like birds flying through the air.

After Heidi had gone to bed that night, she lay awake a little while, thinking over everything the grandmother had said.

If only poor grandmother could hear those comforting words every day!

Suddenly Heidi had an idea.

It excited her so much that she could hardly wait till morning came to start with her plan.

But in her excitement she had forgotten her evening prayer( 祷告词 ).

So, sitting up in bed, she prayed( 祈祷 ) to God.

Then, falling back into the fragrant( 芳香的 ) hay( 干草 ), she soon slept peacefully and soundly( 酣甜地 ) till the bright morning came.

The next evening, Peter paid a visit to Heidi, as usual.

The minute he opened the door she ran up to him, saying,

“Peter, I have to tell you something.”

“Say it,” he replied.

“You must learn to read now,” said the child.

“I have done it already.”

“Yes, yes, Peter, but I don’t mean it that way,” Heidi went on.

“You must learn so that you really know how to read later.”

“I can’t,” Peter remarked( 说 ).

“I’ll teach you, for I know how;

when you have learnt it, you must read one or two songs to grandmother every day.”

“I shan’t!” answered the boy.

This refusal made Heidi very angry.

With flaming( 发火的 ) eyes, she planted( 站定 ) herself before the boy and said,

“I’ll tell you what will happen, if you don’t want to learn.

Your mother has often said that she’ll send you to Frankfurt.

Clara showed me the terrible, large boys’ school there, where you’ll have to go.

There are whole rows of teachers there, and when they are out walking they have high black hats on their heads.

I saw them myself, when I was in Frankfurt!”

Cold shivers( 冷颤 ) ran down Peter’s back.

“Yes, you’ll have to go there,

and when they find out that you can’t read or even spell, they’ll laugh at you!”

“I’ll do it,” said Peter, half angry and half frightened( 害怕的 ).

“Oh, I am glad. Let us start right away!” said Heidi joyfully( 高兴地 ), pulling Peter over to the table.

Among the things that Clara had sent, Heidi had found a little book with the A, B, C and some rhymes( 押韵 ).

She had chosen this for the lessons.

Peter had great difficulty spelling the first rhyme.

Heidi said,

“I’ll read it to you, and then you’ll be able to do it better.”

Then Heidi began to read the letter rhymes to Peter, who learned them unwillingly but quickly.

Thus the days passed by.

In all this time Peter had really made some progress, though the rhymes still gave him difficulty.

Before it was time to go, Peter knew the last letter, and had even begun to read short words.

From this day on he progressed more quickly.

It was three weeks since Heidi had paid her last visit to the grandmother, for much snow had fallen since.

One evening, Peter, coming home, said happily:

“I can do it!”

“What is it you can do, Peter?” asked his mother, eagerly.

“Read.”

“What, is it possible? Did you hear it, grandmother?” said Brigida.

The grandmother also was curious( 好奇的 ) how this had happened.

“I must read a song now; Heidi told me to,” Peter continued.

To the women’s great surprise, Peter began.

After every sentence his mother would shout,

“Who would have ever thought it!” while the grandmother remained silent.
