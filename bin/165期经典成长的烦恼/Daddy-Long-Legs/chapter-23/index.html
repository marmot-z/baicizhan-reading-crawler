Judy获得了一笔丰厚的奖学金，而Daddy-Long-Legs却认为Judy不应该收来自“陌生人”的馈赠，要求她放弃奖学金资格。Judy难以接受，并以激烈的口吻写了回信，表达了自己坚定的立场——绝对不会放弃奖学金！


I’ve chosen economics this year.

When I finish that I’m going to study Charity( 慈善 ) and Reform( 改革 ).

Then, Mr. Trustee( 董事 ), I’ll know just how an Orphans( 孤儿 )’ Home ought to be run.

Don’t you think I’d make a great voter( 投票人 ) if I had my rights?

I was twenty-one last week.

This is a very wasteful country, to throw away such an honest, educated, conscientious( 勤勉认真的 ), intelligent( 聪明的 ) citizen as I would be.

Yours always,

Judy

December 7th

Dear Daddy-Long-Legs,

Thank you for letting me visit Julia—I assume( 假定 ) that your silence means you agree.

The end-of-term dance was last week.

This was the first year that any of us could go; only third- and fourth-years are allowed.

I invited Jimmie McBride, and Sallie invited his friend at Princeton, who visited them last summer at their camp.

Julia invited a man from New York( 纽约，地名 ), who is not very exciting, but socially very superior( 优越的 ).

He is connected with the Chichester family.

Perhaps that means something to you?

It doesn’t mean anything to me.

I found Jimmie a very difficult guest.

He was unhappy because he had only three dances with me.

He said he was shy about dancing with girls he didn’t know!

Anyway, our two days were great fun, and I think the men enjoyed it.

Some of them were quite nervous at first at the thought of facing one thousand girls.

But they got used to it very quickly.

Our two Princeton men had a beautiful time—at least they politely said they had, and they’ve invited us to their dance next spring.

We’ve accepted, so please don’t object, Daddy dear.

Do you want me to tell you a secret that I’ve lately discovered?

And will you promise not to think me vain( 虚荣的 )?

Then listen:

I’m pretty.

I am, really. I’d be stupid not to know it, with three mirrors( 镜子 ) in the room.

A Friend

P.S. This is one of those anonymous( 匿名的 ) letters you read about in novels.

December 20th

Dear Daddy-Long-Legs,

I only have a moment, because I need to catch the four o’clock train.

But I couldn’t go before thanking you for my Christmas box.

I love the furs( 毛皮大衣 ) and the necklace( 项链 ) and the scarf and the gloves and books—and most of all I love you!

But Daddy, you have no business to spoil( 宠坏 ) me this way.

I’m only human—and a girl.

How can I keep my mind on my studies, when you distract( 使…分心 ) me with worldly things?

I think I know now which one of the John Grier Trustees used to give the Christmas tree and the Sunday ice cream( 冰激凌 ).

He was nameless, but I know it was you!

You deserve( 值得 ) to be happy for all the good things you do.

Goodbye, and a very merry Christmas( 圣诞快乐 ).

Yours always,

Judy

January 11th

I meant to write to you from the city, Daddy, but New York kept me very busy.

I had an interesting time and I learned a lot, but I’m glad I don’t come from such a family!

I’m glad I have the John Grier Home for a background.

Whatever problems I had while I was growing up, at least there was no pretense( 虚假 ) about it.

I know now what people mean when they say they are weighed down by Things.

I didn’t draw a deep breath( 呼吸 ) until I was on a train coming back.

All the furniture( 家具 ) was beautiful, and the people I met were beautifully dressed and had perfect manners.

But I never heard one word of real talk from the time we arrived until we left.

I don’t think an idea ever entered the front door.

I only saw Master( 老爷 ) Jervie once when he called at tea time, and then I didn’t have a chance to speak to him alone.

It was really disappointing( 失望的 ) after our nice time last summer.

I don’t think he cares much for his relatives( 家人 )—and I am sure they don’t care much for him! Julia’s mother says he’s not right in the head.

He throws away his money on crazy plans to help the poor, instead of spending it on sensible( 明智的 ) things like expensive sailing boats and cars and racehorses( 赛马 ).

He does buy candy with it though!

He sent Julia and me each a box for Christmas.

I’ve seen lots of theaters( 剧院 ) and hotels and beautiful houses, but I’m glad to get back to college and my books.

I really am a student at heart.

I find this life of study much healthier than that of New York.

College is a very satisfying sort of life.

The books and study and regular( 有规律的 ) classes keep your mind awake.

Then when your mind gets tired, you have the gymnasium( 体育馆 ) and outdoor sport.

And there are always plenty of friends who are thinking about the same things as you are.

We spend a whole evening in nothing but talk, and go to bed with a very cheerful and inspired( 激动人心的 ) feeling.

We feel as though we have solved some major world problems forever( 永远地 ).

And in between, there is always such a lot of nonsense( 胡说 )—just silly jokes about the little things.

It isn’t the great big pleasures that count the most.

It’s making a great deal out of the little ones.

I’ve discovered the true secret of happiness, Daddy, and that is to live in the now.

Not to be always regretting the past, or worrying about the future; but to get the most that you can out of this moment.
