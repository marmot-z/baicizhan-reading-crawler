Master Jervie似乎知道很多有关Daddy-Long-Legs的事情，也希望Judy前往欧洲，这让Judy感到惊讶的同时又觉得古怪。Judy成为了“Monthly”的编辑，但在创作之路上，她走得并不顺利。


It was a very nice, fatherly letter—but frank( 直率的 )!

He said he saw from the address that I was still at college.

His advice was that I should put all of my energy into my lessons, and wait until I graduated( 毕业 ) before beginning to write.

He added his reader’s opinion.

Here it is:

“The author does not yet understand how real people talk and act.

A good deal of humor, but not always in the best of taste.

Tell her to keep on trying, and in time she may produce a real book.”

And I thought I was making a notable addition to American literature( 文学 ).

I was planning to surprise you by writing a great novel( 小说 ) before I graduated.

I collected the material for it while I was at Julia’s last Christmas.

But the editor( 编辑 ) may be right.

Probably two weeks was not long enough to understand the manners and customs( 风俗 ) of a great city.

I burned the book, and went to bed completely discouraged( 气馁的 ).

I thought I was never going to be good for anything, and that you had thrown away your money for nothing.

But what do you think?

I woke up this morning with a beautiful new book idea in my head, and I’ve been going about all day planning my characters, just as happy as I could be.

No one can ever accuse( 指责 ) me of being a pessimist( 悲观主义者 )!

If I had a husband and twelve children swallowed( 吞噬 ) by an earthquake one day, I’d get up the next morning with a smile, and start looking for another set.

Love,

Judy

December 14th

Dear Daddy-Long-Legs,

I had the strangest dream last night.

I thought I went into a book store, and there was a new book called The Life and Letters of Judy Abbott.

It was red, with a picture of the John Grier Home on the cover, and a picture of me inside with “Very truly yours, Judy Abbott,” written below.

But just as I was trying to find out whom I’m going to marry and when I’m going to die, I woke up.

Would you want to read the story of your life—written by an author who knew everything?

What if you could never forget it, but would always know what would happen next, and the exact day and time when you would die?

How many people would have the courage to read it then, and live without hope and without surprises?

Life can be boring sometimes—eat, sleep, repeat.

But imagine how much more boring it would be if nothing unexpected ever happened.

We’re studying philosophy( 哲学 ) now—interesting but hard to get hold of.

I prefer( 更喜欢 ) biology( 生物 ), where you can pin( 把…钉住 ) the subject under discussion to a board.

Do you believe in free will? I do.

I don’t agree at all with the philosophers( 哲学家 ) who think that every action is the inevitable( 不可避免的 ) result of previous( 之前的 ) causes.

I believe in my own free will and my own power to succeed—and that is the belief that moves mountains.

You watch me become a great author!

I have four chapters of my new book finished, and have rough( 大致的 ) versions of five more.

This is a very intellectual( 需要用脑力的 ) sort of letter—does your head hurt, Daddy?

I think I’ll stop now and make some candy.

I’m sorry I can’t send you a piece; it will be unusually good, for we’re going to make it with real cream( 奶油 ) and lots of butter( 黄油 ).

Love,

Judy

P.S. We’re dancing in gymnasium( 体育馆 ) class.

You can see by this picture how much we look like a real ballet( 芭蕾舞团 ).

I am the one at the end, looking so graceful( 优雅的 ).

December 26th

My Dear, Dear, Daddy,

Don’t you have any sense?

Don’t you know that you mustn’t give one girl seventeen Christmas presents?

Think how embarrassing( 尴尬的 ) it would be if we ever quarreled( 吵架 )!

I should have to hire( 租用 ) a moving van( 搬运车 ) to return your gifts.

Thank you, Daddy, a thousand times.

I think you’re the sweetest man that ever lived—and the most foolish( 愚蠢的 )!

Judy

January 9th

Do you wish to do something, Daddy, that will make sure you go to Heaven( 天堂 )?

There is a family here who are in a very bad situation.

They have four children still at home.

The two older boys have disappeared into the world to make their fortune( 发财 ), and have not sent any of it back.

The father worked in a glass factory and got sick—it’s awfully unhealthy work—and now has been sent away to a hospital.

That took all their savings, and the support of the family falls upon the oldest daughter, who is twenty-four.

She makes dresses for $1.50 a day (when she can get the work) and does embroidery( 刺绣 ) in the evening.

The mother isn’t very strong; also, she believes in God, and so refuses to do anything herself.

She sits with her hands folded( 交叉起来 ), while the daughter kills herself with overwork( 工作过度 ) and worry.

She doesn’t see how they are going to get through the rest of the winter—and I don’t either.

One hundred dollars would buy some coal and some shoes for three children so that they could go to school.

And it would leave a little over, so that she needn’t worry herself to death when a few days pass and she doesn’t get work.

You are the richest man I know.

Don’t you suppose you could spare one hundred dollars?

That girl deserves( 值得 ) help a lot more than I ever did.

I wouldn’t ask it except for the girl; I don’t care much what happens to the mother—she is such a jelly-fish.

The way people are forever( 永远 ) looking up to heaven and saying “Perhaps it’s all for the best,” when they are perfectly sure it’s not, makes me so angry.

Fatalism( 宿命论 ), or whatever you choose to call it, is simply another word for laziness.

I’m for a more active religion( 宗教信仰 )!
