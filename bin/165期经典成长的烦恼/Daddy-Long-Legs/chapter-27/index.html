Judy将自己写好的书交给出版社，出版商看完虽然并不觉得写得好，但认为她应该继续创作下去，说不定以后就能写出一本好书。Judy在信中探讨意志的力量，她完全相信人的主观能力，不能把一切都交给所谓的上帝或者命运，那只是软弱与懒惰的另一种说法。


Where do you think my new novel( 小说 ) is?

In the waste basket.

I can see myself that it’s no good, and when a loving author realizes that, what would the public think?

Later

I write to you, Daddy, from a bed of pain.

For two days I’ve been sick with enlarged tonsils( 扁桃体肿大 ).

I can drink hot milk with great difficulty, and that is all I can take.

“Why didn’t your parents have those tonsils out when you were a baby?” the doctor asked.

I’m sure I haven’t any idea, but I doubt if they were thinking much about me.

Yours,

J. A.

Next morning

I just read this over before sending it.

I don’t know why I write such strange, disturbing( 令人不安的 ) things.

Actually( 实际上 ) I am young and happy and full of life; and I trust you are the same.

Youth has nothing to do with birthdays, only with aliveness( 活力 ) of spirit.

So even if your hair is gray, Daddy, you can still be a boy.

Love,

Judy

Jan. 12th

Dear Kind, Generous( 慷慨的 ) Man,

Your check for my family came yesterday.

Thank you so much!

I took it down to them right after lunch, and you should have seen the girl’s face!

She was so surprised and happy that she looked almost young.

She’s only twenty-four.

Isn’t it pitiful?

Anyway, she feels now as though all the good things are coming together.

She has steady( 持续的 ) work ahead for two months—someone’s getting married, and there’s a wedding dress to make.

“Thank the good Lord!” cried the mother, when she understood the fact that that small piece of paper was one hundred dollars.

“It wasn’t the good Lord at all,” I said, “it was Daddy-Long-Legs.” (Mr. Smith, I called you.)

“But it was the good Lord who put it in his mind,” said she.

“Not at all! I put it in his mind myself,” I replied.

But anyway, Daddy, I trust the good Lord will reward( 奖励 ) you.

You deserve ten thousand years out of purgatory( 炼狱 ).

Yours most gratefully( 感激地 ),

Judy Abbott

Sunday

Feb. 15th

Just back from church—a preacher( 牧师 ) from Georgia.

We must take care, he says, not to develop our minds too much at the expense of our emotional( 感情的 ) natures.

It doesn’t matter where these preachers come from, we always get the same sermon( 布道 ).

Why don’t they go to men’s colleges and urge( 敦促 ) the students not to allow their manly natures to be ruined( 被毁灭 ) by too much thinking?

Farewell( 再见 ), kind Sir.

J. Abbott

March Fifth

Dear Mr. Trustee,

Tomorrow is the first Wednesday in the month—a weary( 疲倦的 ) day for the John Grier Home.

How happy they’ll be when five o’clock comes and you pat( 拍 ) them on the head and leave!

Did you ever pat me on the head, Daddy?

I don’t believe so—I only remember fat Trustees( 董事 ).

Give the Home my love, please.

I have quite a feeling of tenderness( 温柔 ) for it as I look back, four years later.

When I first came to college I felt quite angry because I hadn’t had a normal( 正常的 ) childhood.

But now I don’t feel that way in the least.

My childhood has given me a perspective( 观点 ) on the world that other people don’t have.

I know lots of girls (Julia, for instance) who never know that they are happy.

They are so used to the feeling that they don’t notice it.

But I am perfectly sure every moment of my life that I am happy.

And I’m going to keep on being happy, no matter what unpleasant things turn up.

I’m going to treat them (even toothaches( 牙痛 )) as interesting experiences, and be glad to know what they feel like.

However, Daddy, don’t take this love for the J.G.H. too seriously.

If I have five children, I shan’t leave them on the steps of an Orphans( 孤儿 )’ Home in order to make sure they are brought up simply.

Give my kindest regards( 问候 ) to Mrs. Lippett (that, I think, is truthful; love would be a little strong) and don’t forget to tell her what a beautiful nature I’ve developed.

Love,

Judy

LOCK WILLOW( 柳树 ),

April 4th

Dear Daddy,

Notice the address.

Sallie and I are spending Easter Vacation here.

We decided that the best thing we could do with our ten days was to find a quiet place.

We are walking over the hills and reading and writing, and having a nice, restful time.

We climbed to the top of Sky Hill this morning, where Master( 老爷 ) Jervie and I once cooked dinner.

It doesn’t seem possible that it was nearly two years ago.

I could still see the place where the smoke of our fire blackened the rock.

It is funny how certain places get connected with certain people, and you never go back without thinking of them.

I was quite lonely without him—for two minutes.

What do you think is my latest activity, Daddy?

You will begin to believe that I never learn—I am writing another book.

I started it three weeks ago and am eating it up in chunks( 大块 ).

I’ve understood the secret. Master Jervie and that editor( 编辑 ) were right; you are most convincing( 令人信服的 ) when you write about the things you know.

And this time it is about something that I do know—very well.

Guess where it’s set?

In the John Grier Home!

And it’s good, Daddy—it’s just about the tiny little things that happened every day.

I’m a realist now.

I shall go back to romance( 爱情故事 ) when my own exciting future begins.

This new book is going to get itself finished—and published( 被出版 )!

You’ll see!

If you just want a thing hard enough and keep on trying, you do get it in the end.

I’ve been trying for four years to get a letter from you—and I haven’t given up hope yet.

Goodbye, Daddy dear.

Love,

Judy
