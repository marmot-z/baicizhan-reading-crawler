Judy将Daddy-Long-Legs的100美元转交给了那户不幸的家庭，并极力驳斥了女主人认为是上帝在保佑他们的观点。Judy曾经非常讨厌自己长大的孤儿院，现在她改变了看法，因为她觉得在那儿长大的日子给了她别人所没有的世界观。不仅如此，那些珍贵的记忆都可以被她记录下来，当做写作的素材，这也会使得她更有说服力。


May 17th

Dear Daddy-Long-Legs,

This is going to be very short because my shoulder hurts at the sight of a pen.

Taking lecture( 讲座 ) notes all day, and working on my novel( 小说 ) all evening, make too much writing.

We will have our graduation ceremony( 毕业典礼 ) in three weeks from next Wednesday.

I think you might come and meet me—I shall hate you if you don’t!

Julia’s inviting Master( 老爷 ) Jervie, since he’s a relative, and Sallie’s inviting Jimmie McBride, for the same reason, but who is there for me to invite?

Just you and Lippett, and I don’t want her. Please come.

Yours, with love and aching( 疼痛的 ) fingers,

Judy

LOCK WILLOW( 柳树 ),

June 19th

Dear Daddy-Long-Legs,

I’m educated! My diploma( 毕业证书 ) is in the bottom drawer( 抽屉 ) with my two best dresses.

Thank you for your roses.

They were lovely.

Master Jervie and Jimmie both gave me roses, too, but I left theirs in water at home and carried yours at the graduation ceremony.

Here I am at Lock Willow for the summer—perhaps forever( 永远的 ).

It’s peaceful, and it’s easy to write here.

What more does an author want?

I am mad about my book.

I think of it every waking moment, and dream of it at night.

All I want is peace and quiet and lots of time to work (and good meals, of course).

I’d be expecting to have you come and visit—only I know now that that is hopeless.

When you wouldn’t come to my graduation, I tore( 扯出 ) you from my heart and buried( 埋葬 ) you forever.

Judy Abbott, Bachelor of Arts( 文科学士 )

July 24th

Dearest Daddy-Long-Legs,

Isn’t it fun to work—or don’t you ever do it?

It’s especially fun when that work is the thing you’d rather do than anything else in the world.

I’ve been writing as fast as my pen would go every day this summer.

My only problem with life is that the days aren’t long enough to write all the beautiful and valuable and fun thoughts I’m thinking.

I’ve finished the second draft( 草稿 ) of my book, and am going to begin the third tomorrow morning at half past seven.

It’s the sweetest book you ever saw—it is, truly.

I think of nothing else.

I can hardly wait in the morning to dress and eat before beginning; then I write and write and write till suddenly I’m exhausted( 筋疲力尽的 ).

Then I go out with Colin (the new sheep dog) and run through the fields and get a fresh supply of ideas for the next day.

It’s the most beautiful book you ever saw—Oh, sorry—I said that before.

You don’t think that I am too proud of my work, do you, Daddy dear?

I’m not, really, only just now I’m in the enthusiastic( 狂热的 ) stage.

Maybe later on I’ll get cold and critical( 批判的 ).

No, I’m sure I won’t!

This time I’ve written a real book.

Just wait till you see it.

I still love you, Daddy dear, and I’m very happy.

With beautiful scenery all about, and lots to eat, and a comfortable bed and plenty of paper and lots of ink( 墨水 )—what more does one want in the world?

Yours as always,

Judy

PS. The postman arrives with some more news.

We are to expect Master Jervie next Friday.

He will be staying for a week.

That’s something to look forward to—only I am afraid my poor book will suffer( 受苦 ).

Master Jervie is very demanding.

August 27th

Dear Daddy-Long-Legs,

Where are you, I wonder?

I never know what part of the world you are in, but I hope you’re not in New York( 纽约，地名 ) during this awful weather.

I hope you’re on a mountaintop( 山顶 ) (but not in Switzerland( 瑞士，地名 ); somewhere nearer) looking at the snow and thinking about me.

Please be thinking about me.

I’m quite lonely and I want to be thought about.

Oh, Daddy, I wish I knew you!

Then when we were unhappy we could cheer each other up.

I don’t think I can stand much more of Lock Willow.

I’m thinking of moving.

Sallie is going to do charity( 慈善 ) work with the poor in Boston next winter.

Don’t you think it would be nice for me to go with her, and then we could get an apartment( 公寓 ) together?

I would write while she did her charity work, and we could be together in the evenings.

Evenings are very long when there’s no one but the Semples and Carrie and Amasai to talk to.

I know already that you won’t like my idea.

I can read your secretary’s letter now:

“To Miss Jerusha Abbott.

“DEAR MADAM( 女士 ),

“Mr. Smith prefers( 更愿意 ) that you remain at Lock Willow.

“Yours truly,

“ELMER H. GRIGGS.”

I hate your secretary!

But truly, Daddy, I think I shall have to go to Boston.

I can’t stay here.

If something doesn’t happen soon, I shall kill myself.

And it’s too hot here. It hasn’t rained for weeks and weeks.

This letter sounds as if I have gone crazy, but I haven’t. I just want some family.

Goodbye, my dearest Daddy.

I wish I knew you.

Judy

LOCK WILLOW,

September 19th

Dear Daddy,

Something has happened and I need advice.

I need it from you, and from nobody else in the world.

Wouldn’t it be possible for me to see you?

It’s so much easier to talk than to write; and I’m afraid your secretary might open the letter.

Judy

P.S. I’m very unhappy.

LOCK WILLOW,

October 3rd

Dear Daddy-Long-Legs,

Your note in your own (shaky) handwriting came this morning.

I am so sorry that you have been ill.

I wouldn’t have bothered( 打扰 ) you with my problems if I had known.

Yes, I will tell you the trouble, but it’s sort of complicated( 复杂的 ) to write, and very private.

Please don’t keep this letter, but burn it.

Before I begin—here’s a check for one thousand dollars.

It seems funny, doesn’t it, for me to be sending a check to you?

Where do you think I got it?
