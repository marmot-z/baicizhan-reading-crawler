奶奶来了，看见Clara不仅健康了许多，还能在帮助下走路，非常开心，也对爷爷十分感激，并让Peter送信给Mr. Sesemann，让他亲眼看看这个场景。谁知Mr. Sesemann也想给他们惊喜，提早结束工作，已经在山上了。下山途中，Peter遇到了Mr. Sesemann，以为他是来抓人的警察，匆忙逃走，却不慎滚落下山，遍体鳞伤。


Mr. Sesemann, climbing further, at last saw his goal( 目标 ) before him.

He eagerly( 心切地 ) hurried to see the daughter he loved so much.

They had seen him long ago from the hut, and a treat was prepared for him that he never expected.

As he made the last steps, he saw two forms coming towards him.

A tall girl, with light hair and red face, was holding on to Heidi, whose dark eyes shone with delight( 高兴 ).

Mr. Sesemann stopped short, staring( 盯 ) at this scene.

Suddenly big tears( 眼泪 ) rushed from his eyes, for this fair girl before him looked the same as Clara’s mother.

Mr. Sesemann at this moment did not know if he was awake or dreaming.

“Papa, don’t you know me anymore?” Clara called with beaming( 眉开眼笑的 ) eyes.

“Have I changed so much?”

Mr. Sesemann rushed up to her, folding( 交叠 ) her in his arms.

“Yes, you have changed. How is it possible? Is it really true? Is it really you, Clara?”

asked the overjoyed( 欣喜若狂的 ) father, hugging( 拥抱 ) her again and again.

His mother joined them now, for she wanted to see the happiness of her son.

“What do you say to this, my son? Isn’t our surprise finer than yours?” she greeted( 同...打招呼 ) him.

“But come over to the one who helped us now—I mean the uncle.”

“Yes, indeed, I also must greet our little Heidi and the Uncle,” said the gentleman, shaking Heidi’s hand.

“Well? Always fresh and happy on the mountain?

I guess I don’t need to ask, for no Alpine( 高山的 ) rose can look healthier.

Ah, child, what joy this is to me!”

With beaming eyes, the child looked at the kind gentleman who had always been so good to her.

While the two men, who had at last approached each other, were conversing( 交谈 ), grandmama walked over to the trees.

There, under the fir( 冷杉 ) trees, another surprise awaited( 等待 ) her.

A lot of beautiful blue flowers stood as if they had grown there.

“How excellent, how wonderful! What a sight!” she shouted, clapping( 拍 ) her hands.

“Heidi, come here! Have you brought me those? Oh, they are beautiful!”

The children had joined her. Heidi told her that it was another person’s deed( 行为 ).

At that moment a sound was heard, and they saw Peter, who was trying to go up behind the trees to avoid the hut.

Immediately the old lady called to him, for she thought that Peter himself had picked the flowers for her.

He must be creeping( 爬 ) away out of modesty( 谦逊 ), the kind lady thought.

To give him his reward( 报酬 ), she called:

“Come here, my boy! Don’t be afraid.”

With his hair standing up on end, Peter approached.

“Come straight to me, boy,” the old lady encouraged him.

“Now tell me, boy, if you have done that.”

In his anxiety( 焦急 ), Peter did not see the grandmama’s finger that pointed to the flowers.

He only saw the uncle standing near the hut, watching him closely,

and beside him was the policeman, the person he was most afraid of.

Trembling( 颤抖 ) in his whole body, Peter answered,

“Yes!”

“Well, but what are you so frightened( 感到害怕的 ) about?”

“Because—because it is broken and can never be mended( 被修补 ) again,” Peter said, with his knees shaking under him.

The grandmama now walked over to the hut:

“My dear uncle,” she asked kindly,

“is this poor boy out of his mind?”

“Not at all,” was the reply;

“only the boy was the wind which blew away the wheelchair.

He is expecting the punishment he well deserves( 应得 ).”

Grandmama was very much surprised, for she thought that Peter looked like he would not do something like that.

Why should he have destroyed( 破坏 ) the chair?

The uncle told her that he had noticed many signs of anger in the boy since Clara had arrived on the Alp.

He told the grandmama that he had suspected( 怀疑 ) the boy from the beginning.

“My dear uncle,” the old lady said,

“we must not punish him further. We must be just.

It was very hard on him when Clara took Heidi from him, who is and was his greatest treasure.

When he had to sit alone day after day, he got angrier and angrier, which drove him to this bad act.

It was rather foolish( 愚蠢的 ), but we all get so when we get angry.”

The lady walked over to the boy again, who was still trembling with fear.

Sitting down on the bench( 长凳 ), she began:

“Come, Peter, I’ll tell you something.

Stop trembling and listen. You pushed the chair down, to destroy it.

You knew very well that it was bad and deserved punishment.

But you have made a mistake by thinking that you would hurt Clara by destroying her chair.

It has happened that what you have done has been the greatest good for her.

She would probably never have tried to walk, if her chair had been there.

Now tell me if you have a wish, my boy, for I am going to give you something to remember your friends from Frankfurt.

What is it? What would you like to have?”

Peter, lifting his head, stared at the grandmama with round, astonished( 震惊的 ) eyes.

He would get rewarded instead of punished!
