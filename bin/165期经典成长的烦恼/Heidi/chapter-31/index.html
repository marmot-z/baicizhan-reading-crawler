Heidi now called to him to come down, but as reply he only said,

“Shan’t come.”

“But you must; come quickly, for I want you to help me. Quickly!” urged( 催促 ) the child.

“Don’t want to,” was the reply.

Heidi hurried up the mountain now and shouted angrily to the boy:

“Peter, if you don’t come this minute, I shall do something that you won’t like.”

Those words scared( 使惊吓 ) Peter, for he knew he had done something wrong.

He had thought himself safe till this moment, when Heidi seemed to talk as if she knew it all.

What if the grandfather should hear about it!

Shaking with fear, Peter obeyed( 听从 ).

“I shall only come if you promise not to do what you said,” insisted( 坚持 ) the boy.

“No, no, I won’t. Don’t be afraid,” said Heidi.

“Just come along; it isn’t so hard.”

Peter, on approaching Clara, was told to help raise the child from the ground on one side, while Heidi helped on the other.

This went easily enough, but difficulties soon followed.

Clara was not able to stand alone, and how could they get any further?

“You must put one arm around my neck, and put the other around Peter’s and lean( 靠 ) firmly upon it,

then we shall be able to carry you!” said Heidi.

The boy, who had never offered his arm to anybody in his life,

had to be shown how to do so first, before further efforts could be made.

But it was too hard. Clara tried to set her feet forward, but she lost hope.

“Press your feet on the ground more and I am sure it will hurt you less,” suggested Heidi.

“Do you think so?” said Clara, nervously.

But, obeying, she put her foot on the ground more firmly and soon another, giving a little cry as she went.

“Oh, it really has hurt me less,” she said joyfully( 高兴地 ).

“Try it again,” Heidi urged her.

Clara did, and took another step, and then another, and another still.

Suddenly she cried aloud:

“Oh, Heidi, I can do it. Oh, I really can. Just look! I can take steps, one after another.”

Heidi exclaimed( 大声喊叫 ):

“Oh, Clara, can you really? Can you take steps now? Oh, if only grandfather would come!

Now you can walk, Clara, now you can walk,” she kept on saying joyfully.

Clara held on tight( 紧紧地 ) to the children, but with every new step she became more firm.

“Now you can come up here every day,” cried Heidi.

“Now we can walk wherever we want to, and you don’t have to be pushed in a chair anymore.

Now you’ll be able to walk all your life. Oh, what joy!”

Clara’s greatest wish, to be well like other people, had been fulfilled( 实现 ) at last.

The two children could hardly believe this happiness that had come to them.

It filled their hearts and made them silent.

Thus the hours flew, and the day was long past noon.

Soon after, the grandfather was seen climbing up the Alp.

Heidi ran to meet him, telling him of the great event.

The old man’s face shone at this news. Going over to Clara, he said,

“So you have risked it? Now we have won.”

Then picking her up, he put one arm around her waist( 腰 ), and he held out the other one as support.

With his help she marched more firmly than ever.

Heidi jumped happily by their side.

In all this excitement the grandfather did not lose his judgement( 判断 ), and before long he lifted Clara on his arm to carry her home.

He knew that tiring her too much would be dangerous, and rest was needed for the girl.

Peter, arriving in the village late that day, saw many people talking together.

They were all standing about an interesting object, and everybody pushed and fought for a chance to get nearest.

It was no other than the chair.

“I saw it when they carried it up,” Peter heard the baker( 面包师 ) say.

“I am sure it was worth a lot of money. I should just like to know how it has happened.

“When the gentleman from Frankfurt hears what has happened,

he’ll surely find out all about it, and I should pity whoever did it.

I am glad I haven’t been up on the Alp for so long! Otherwise they might suspect( 怀疑 ) me.”

Many more opinions were shared, but Peter had heard enough.

He quietly slipped away( 溜走 ) and went home.

What if they should find out he had done it?

A policeman might arrive any time now and they might take him away to prison( 监狱 ).

Peter’s hair stood up on end at this alarming( 令人忧虑的 ) thought.

He was so troubled when he came home that he did not answer any questions and even refused his dish of potatoes.

Hurriedly, he got into bed.

Next morning a letter was written to grandmama, inviting her to come up to the Alp within a week.

The children had planned to surprise her with the news.

Clara hoped then to be able to walk alone, with Heidi as her guide.

The following days were happier still for Clara.

She progressed, and took longer walks every day.

She got hungrier every day, and the grandfather had to make larger slices( 片 ) of the bread and butter( 黄油 ) that, to his delight( 高兴 ), disappeared so rapidly.

He had to fill bowl after bowl of the warm milk for the hungry children.

In that way they reached the end of the week that was to bring the grandmama.
