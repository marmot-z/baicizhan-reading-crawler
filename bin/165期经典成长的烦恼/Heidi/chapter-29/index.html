Heidi带着Clara看了周围的花，Clara非常欢喜。吃午饭的时候，Clara居然比平时多吃了一块奶酪，爷爷和奶奶都认为Clara在这里能够恢复健康。爷爷提出让Clara住在Heidi的小房间里，为她铺了一个又软又舒服的床，Clara在这里住下了……


The sun was just rising.

Clara, having just a moment ago opened her eyes, looked about her in amazement( 惊讶 ).

Bright sunshine danced on her bed.

She had never slept better in all her life.

Heidi was soon awake, too, and lost no time in coming down to join Clara, who was already sitting in the sun.

A cool morning wind touched their cheeks( 脸颊 ), and the smell of the fir( 冷杉 ) trees filled their lungs( 肺 ) with every breath( 呼吸 ).

Clara had never breathed such pure( 纯净的 ), cool morning air and never felt such warm, delicious sunshine on her feet and hands.

It was better than she had ever dreamed.

The grandfather was just coming out of the shed( 棚 ) with two full bowls of warm, snow-white milk.

Handing one to each of the children, he said to Clara,

“This will do you good, little girl. It comes from Schwänli and will give you strength.”

At first Clara had hesitated( 犹豫 ) a little,

but when she saw that Heidi’s bowl was nearly empty already, she drank without stopping.

Oh, how good it was! It tasted like sugar.

“We’ll drink two tomorrow,” said the grandfather.

After their breakfast, Peter arrived.

Taking the uncle’s two goats, he started off quickly, angrily looking back now and then( 时不时地 ).

As Heidi had followed a little way, Peter called to her:

“You must come along, Heidi.”

“No, but I can’t,” Heidi called back.

“I won’t be able to come as long as Clara is with me.

Grandfather has promised, though, to let us come up with you one day.”

With those words Heidi returned to Clara, while the goatherd( 牧羊人 ) was hurrying on, angrily shaking his fists( 拳头 ).

The children had promised to write a letter to grandmama every day, so they immediately started on their task.

Heidi brought out her own little chair, her school books and her papers.

With the books and the papers on Clara’s lap( 大腿 ), they began to write.

The morning had passed, and lunch was ready.

They again ate outside, because Clara had to be in the open air all day.

The afternoon was spent in the cool shadow( 树荫处 ) of the fir trees.

It was not long before Peter arrived with his goats,

but without even answering the girls’ friendly greeting, he disappeared quickly with an angry look on his face.

While Schwänli was being milked in the shed, Clara said,

“Oh, Heidi, I feel as if I could not wait for my milk.

Isn’t it funny? All my life I have only eaten because I had to.

And now I am so hungry!”

When at last the full bowls were brought by the old man,

Clara eagerly drank the contents( 碗里盛的东西 ) in one time and even finished before Heidi.

“Please, may I have a little more?” she asked, holding out the bowl.

Nodding, much pleased, the grandfather soon refilled it.

This time he also brought a slice of( 一片 ) bread with him and butter( 黄油 ) for the children.

This evening Clara fell asleep the moment she lay down.

Two or three days passed in this pleasant way.

The next brought a surprise.

Two strong porters( 门房 ) came up the Alp, each carrying on his back a fresh, white bed.

They also brought a letter from grandmama.

She thanked the children for their faithful( 坚持的 ) writing, and told them that the beds were meant for them.

When they went to sleep that night, they found their new beds in exactly the same position as their former ones had been.

The grandfather took excellent care of his little patient.

Every day he climbed around to find the best food for Schwänli, so that her milk would be extra( 额外的 ) healthy.

It was the third week of Clara’s stay.

Every morning after the grandfather had carried her down, he said to her,

“Would my Clara try to stand a little?” Clara always sighed( 叹息 ),

“Oh, it hurts me so!”

but he made her stand a little longer every day, though she would hold on to him.

Heidi had told Clara over and over again of all the flowers on the pasture( 牧场 ),

of the masses of golden roses and the blue flowers that covered the ground.

She had just been telling it again, when a longing seized( 抓住 ) her.

She jumped up and ran over to her grandfather, who was busy in his workshop( 工作间 ).

“Oh, grandfather,” she cried from far away,

“won’t you come with us to the pasture tomorrow? Oh, it’s so beautiful up there now.”

“All right, I will,” he replied;

“but tell Clara that she must do something to please me;

she must try to stand longer this evening for me.”

Heidi merrily( 欢乐地 ) came running with her message.

Of course, Clara promised, for it was her greatest wish to go up with Heidi to the pasture!

When Peter returned this evening, he heard of the plan for the next day.

But for answer Peter only made a low sound, nearly hitting poor Thistlefinch in his anger.

The next day started cloudless and fair.

The grandfather was still with the children, when Peter came climbing up;

his goats kept at a good distance from him, to get away from the rod( 杆 ), which was striking right and left.

The truth was that the boy was terribly angry by the changes that had come.

When he passed the hut in the morning, Heidi was always busy with the strange child, and in the evening it was the same.

All summer long Heidi had not been up with him for a single time; it was too much!

And today she was coming at last, but again in company with this hateful stranger.
