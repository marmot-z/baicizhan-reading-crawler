Judy即将毕业，她邀请Daddy-Long-Legs前来参加自己的毕业典礼。Judy寄给Daddy-Long-Legs一张1000美元的支票，她是怎样得到这么多钱的？


I’ve sold my story, Daddy.

It’s going to be published( 被出版 ) in seven parts in a magazine, and then in a book!

You might think I’d be wild with joy( 喜悦 ), but I’m not.

I don’t care.

Of course I’m glad to begin paying you—I owe( 欠 ) you over two thousand more.

It’s coming, one part at a time.

Now please don’t make any difficulties about accepting it, because it makes me happy to return it.

I owe you a great deal more than just the money.

I will continue to pay the rest of it all my life in gratitude( 感激 ) and affection( 喜爱 ).

And now, Daddy, about the other thing; please give me your advice, whether you think I’ll like it or not.

You know that I’ve always had a very special feeling towards you.

I feel that in a way you are my whole family.

But you won’t mind, will you, if I tell you that I have a very much more special feeling for another man?

You can probably guess without much trouble who he is.

My letters have been very full of Master Jervie for a very long time.

I wish I could make you understand what he is like and how good we are together.

We think the same about most things—and when we don’t, I am afraid I sometimes change my ideas to match his!

But he is almost always right.

He ought to be, since he’s fourteen years older.

In other ways, though, he’s just an overgrown boy( 大孩子 ) who needs looking after.

He doesn’t have any sense about wearing rubber boots( 橡胶靴子 ) when it rains.

He and I always think the same things are funny, and that’s important.

It’s terrible when two people’s senses of humor don’t match.

I believe there’s no way to get over that!

He is just himself, and I miss him.

The whole world seems empty without him.

I hate the moonlight because it’s beautiful and he isn’t here to see it with me.

But maybe you’ve loved somebody, too, and you know?

If you have, I don’t need to explain; if you haven’t, I can’t explain.

Anyway, that’s the way I feel—and I’ve refused to marry him.

I didn’t tell him why.

I couldn’t think of anything to say.

And now he has gone away, imagining that I want to marry Jimmie McBride.

I don’t: Jimmie isn’t grown up enough.

But Master Jervie and I had some misunderstandings, and we both hurt each other’s feelings( 感情 ).

The reason I sent him away was not because I didn’t care for him, but because I cared for him so much.

I was afraid he would regret it in the future—and I couldn’t stand that!

It didn’t seem right for a person of my background to marry into any such family as his.

I never told him about the Home, and I hated to explain that I didn’t know who I was.

I may be dreadful( 糟糕的 ), you know.

And his family are proud—and I’m proud, too!

Also, after having been educated as a writer, I owe it to you to at least try to be one.

It wouldn’t be fair to accept your education and then go off and not use it.

But now that I have sold a book and am going to be able to pay back the money, I feel that I have paid you back a little.

Besides, I suppose I could keep on being a writer even if I did marry.

It’s possible to be both a writer and a wife.

I’ve been thinking very hard about it.

He has unusual ideas.

Maybe he wouldn’t mind marrying a poor orphan( 孤儿 ) girl so much as some men might.

Perhaps when two people are always happy together and lonely when they are apart, they ought not to let anything in the world stand between them.

Of course I want to believe that!

But I’d like to get your honest, objective opinion.

You probably have a Family also, and will look at it from a worldly point of view and not just an emotional( 感情的 ), human point of view.

So you see how brave I am to lay it before you.

Suppose I go to him and explain that the trouble isn’t Jimmie, but is the John Grier Home—would that be a dreadful thing for me to do?

It would take a great deal of courage.

I’d almost rather be unhappy for the rest of my life.

This happened nearly two months ago; I haven’t heard a word from him since he left.

I was just getting sort of used to the feeling of a broken heart, when a letter came from Julia that stirred me all up( 惹我伤心 ) again.

She said—very casually—that “Uncle Jervis” had been caught out all night in a storm, and had been ill ever since with pneumonia( 肺炎 ).

And I never knew it.

I was feeling hurt because he had just disappeared without a word.

I think he’s pretty unhappy, and I know I am!

What seems to you the right thing for me to do?

Judy

October 6th

Dearest Daddy-Long-Legs,

Yes, certainly I’ll come—at half past four next Wednesday afternoon.

Of course I can find the way.

I’ve been in New York( 纽约，地名 ) three times and am not quite a baby.

I can’t believe that I am really going to see you.

I’ve been just thinking about you so long that it hardly seems as though you are a real flesh-and-blood person.

You are awfully good, Daddy, to bother( 打扰 ) yourself with me, when you’re not strong.

Take care and don’t catch cold.

These fall rains are very damp( 潮湿的 ).

Love,

Judy

P. S. I’ve just had an awful thought.

Do you have a butler( 男管家 )?

I’m afraid of butlers, and if one opens the door I shall faint( 晕过去 ) upon the step.

What can I say to him?

You didn’t tell me your name.

Shall I ask for Mr. Smith?
