Peter以为Heidi知道了他的所作所为，害怕被告发，于是便帮助Clara行走，过程中，Clara发现自己居然可以走一点儿路程，Heidi和她都非常高兴。他们决定请奶奶过来，给她一个惊喜。下山的Peter听见人们讨论轮椅的事之后非常担心，回家后没有吃完饭就爬上了床……


A day before her visit, the grandmama had sent a letter to announce( 通知 ) her coming.

Peter brought it up with him next morning.

The grandfather was already before the hut with the children and his merry( 欢乐的 ) goats.

His face looked proud, as he looked at the red faces of the girls and the shining hair of his two goats.

Peter, approaching, neared the uncle slowly.

As soon as he had delivered( 投递 ) the letter, he sprang back shyly, looking about him as if he was afraid.

Then with a jump he started off.

All the way Peter was taken by fear.

He could not help thinking of the policeman who was coming from Frankfurt( 法兰克福，地名 ) to bring him to prison( 监狱 ).

It was a busy morning for Heidi, who put the hut in order for the expected visitor.

The grandfather also returned from a walk, on which he had gathered( 聚集 ) a lot of glorious( 美丽的 ) deep blue flowers.

The children, who were sitting near the house, shouted for joy( 欢乐 ) when they saw the flowers.

Heidi, getting up from time to time to look down the path( 小路 ),

suddenly discovered grandmama, sitting on a white horse and accompanied( 陪伴 ) by two men.

The party came nearer and nearer, and soon reached the top.

“What do I see? Clara, what is this? Why are you not sitting in your chair? How is this possible?”

cried the grandmama in alarm( 慌张 ), while she quickly got off her horse.

Before she had quite reached the children, she threw her arms up in great excitement:

“Clara, is that really you? You have a red, round cheek( 脸颊 ), my child! I hardly know you anymore!”

Grandmama was going to rush at her grandchild, when Heidi was getting up, and Clara, taking her arm, got up too.

They quietly took a little walk.

The grandmama was rooted to the spot( 呆住不动 ) from fear.

What was this? Straightly and firmly, Clara walked beside her friend.

When they came back, their red faces beamed( 眉开眼笑 ).

Rushing toward the children, the grandmama hugged( 拥抱 ) them over and over again.

Then the grandmama looked at the uncle, who sat on the bench( 长凳 ) smiling.

Taking Clara’s arm in hers, she walked over to him, continually expressing her delight( 高兴 ).

When she reached the old man, she took both his hands in hers and said,

“My dear, dear uncle! How much we have to thank you for! This is your work, your care and nursing—”

Then Clara called,

“Yes, and also Schwänli’s good, delicious milk.

Grandmama, you ought to see how much goat milk I can drink now; oh, it is so good!”

“Indeed, I can see that from your cheeks,” said the grandmama, smiling.

“I hardly recognize( 认出 ) you anymore. Now I must telegraph( 给...发电报 ) your father to come.

But I shan’t tell him anything about you, for it will be the greatest joy of all his life.

My dear uncle, how are we going to manage it?

Have you sent the two men who accompanied me away?”

“I have, but I can easily send the goatherd( 牧羊人 ).”

So they decided that Peter should take the message.

The uncle immediately whistled( 吹口哨 ) so loud that it could be heard everywhere.

Soon Peter arrived, white with fear, for he thought the grandfather had found out what he had done.

But he only received a paper that was to be carried to the post office of the village.

Relieved( 放松的 ) for the moment, Peter set out.

Meanwhile( 同时 ) Mr. Sesemann, having finished his business in Paris( 巴黎，地名 ), was also preparing a surprise.

Without writing to his mother, he traveled to Ragatz on a sunny summer morning.

He had arrived on this very day, some hours after his mother’s departure( 离开 ).

And now, taking a carriage, he drove to Mayenfeld.

The long way up to the Alp from there seemed very tiring and far to the traveler.

When would he reach the grandfather’s hut?

There were many little roads branching( 分叉 ) off in several directions,

and sometimes Mr. Sesemann doubted if he had taken the right path.

Standing still and cooling his face, he saw a boy running down the hill at topmost speed.

Mr. Sesemann called to him, but with no success, for the boy kept at a shy distance.

“Now, my boy, can’t you tell me if I am on the right path to the hut

where Heidi lives and the people from Frankfurt are staying?”

A low sound of terror( 惊恐 ) was the only reply.

Peter shot off and rushed head over heels( 用尽全力地 ) down the mountain side.

“What a funny, shy mountaineer!” Mr. Sesemann remarked( 说 ) to himself.

After watching the boy going down a little while, he soon went on his way.

Despite the greatest effort, Peter could not stop himself, and kept rolling down( 滚下 ) the mountain.

But his fright( 害怕 ) was still more terrible than the pain.

This stranger was the policeman—that was a certain fact!

At last Peter was caught in a bush( 灌木丛 ), and was able to keep himself from falling any farther.

Peter got back to his feet.

He longed to go home to bed and hide, for he only felt safe there.

But he had to go up to the goats, and the uncle had clearly told him to come back as quickly as he could.

Groaning( 呻吟 ), he walked up to the Alp.

How could he run now, with his fear and his poor, painful body?
